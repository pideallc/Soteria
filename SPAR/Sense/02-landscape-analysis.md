## 1. The Core Issues: Changing the Threat Model

Mainstream cybersecurity is built around protecting corporate networks and state secrets. It assumes the adversary is a faceless, external hacker and the goal is an impenetrable digital fortress. **Feminist cybersecurity argues this model is fundamentally broken for women and marginalized groups.**

### The "User is the Weakest Link" Fallacy

Traditional tech blames the user for clicking a bad link or using a weak password. Feminist cybersecurity rejects this: **if a system requires a user to jump through overly complex, jargony hoops to stay safe, the design is the failure, not the user.**

### Gendered Threat Models

Women face vastly different digital threats than the standard corporate model accounts for:
- Technology-facilitated gender-based violence (GBV)
- Intimate partner surveillance (stalkerware)
- Doxxing
- Targeted identity theft

### The Burden of Labor

Currently, the burden of digital safety is placed almost entirely on the individual victim, requiring them to become impromptu IT experts to protect themselves and their families.

## 2. The Academics: Reconceptualizing Security

The academic focus is on shifting the paradigm from **"security as isolation"** to **"security as communal care."**

### The Reconfigure Network (Oxford Internet Institute)

Researchers like **Julia Slupska** and **Gina Neff** have done work on "Feminist Action Research in Cybersecurity." Key findings:

- Women demonstrate profound care and thoughtfulness regarding the digital privacy of their families and communities
- This directly validates the hypothesis about "super users"
- They advocate for "everyday cybersecurity," treating users as assets with agency rather than security liabilities

### Technofeminist Imaginaries

Academics are pushing for a **"positive security" model**: instead of just playing defense against patriarchal violence and scams, how can technology actively foster safe, joyful, and autonomous online experiences?

## 3. The Pioneers & Activists: The Ground Game

### Hackblossom & Noah Kelley
- Creators of the **DIY Guide to Feminist Cybersecurity**
- Focus on demystifying technical jargon
- Use technology to cultivate solidarity and personal autonomy

### Chayn
- Global non-profit run by survivors and allies
- Creates open-source tech to address gender-based violence
- Their DIY Online Safety guides are masterclasses in **trauma-informed, highly accessible cybersecurity UX** for women who may be actively compromised

### The Association for Progressive Communications (APC)
- Drafted the **Feminist Principles of the Internet**
- Frames digital privacy not just as a feature, but as a **fundamental human right** necessary to protect women's bodies, speech, and activism from surveillance

## 4. Academic Focus Groups (Identified Later in Conversation)

The academics and activists generally focus on a **"Coalition of the Vulnerable."** The discourse breaks the focus into these high-stakes groups:

| Group | Primary Threat Vectors | Academic Focus |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Survivors of Intimate Partner Violence (IPV)** | Stalkerware, domestic surveillance | The domestic environment as a site of surveillance |
| **Women Human Rights Defenders (WHRDs) & Activists** | State-sponsored doxxing, Pegasus-style spyware, identity tracking | Oppressive regime resistance |
| **LGBTQ+ Communities** | "Outing" as a weapon, safety of digital "third spaces" | Platform safety, identity protection |
| **Sex Workers** | Financial exclusion (FOSTA-SESTA), shadowbanning, physical safety via doxxing | Multi-vector attack intersection |

### Why Sex Workers Sit at the Center

Sex workers face **the most complex, multi-vector attacks** — including financial exclusion, platform-level shadowbanning, and physical safety risks via doxxing. They sit at the intersection of almost every digital threat identified in the landscape.

> "If you solve for a sex worker, you are solving for a user who faces simultaneous financial, legal, physical, and social threats. A system that can successfully hide a sex worker's identity from a state actor, a banking algorithm, and a physical stalker all at once is, by definition, the most secure system ever built."
